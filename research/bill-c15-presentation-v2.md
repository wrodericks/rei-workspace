# Bill C-15 — ICA Amendments
## 4-Minute Presentation | Insurance Finance & Risk Audience
## v2 — Conversational

---

So Bill C-15 — the Budget 2025 Implementation Act — got Royal Assent on March 26th. There are four divisions that touch the Insurance Companies Act, and I want to walk through what actually changed.

---

**Division 10 — Sunset Extension**

This one's straightforward. The ICA has always required Parliament to periodically reauthorize federally regulated insurers to keep operating. C-15 pushes that deadline to June 30, 2033. No operational impact — it's housekeeping. But it was coming up and now it's not.

---

**Division 11 — Investment and Lending Limits**

This is the one that actually matters.

The ICA used to have hard statutory limits on how much an insurer could put into commercial loans, real property, and equity investments. Every type of entity — life companies, P&C, societies, foreign branches, holding companies — had its own set of ceilings, either written into the statute or set by regulation.

All of that has been repealed.

What replaces it is OSFI discretion. If the Superintendent decides a company's exposure to commercial loans, real property, or equity poses a prudential risk, they can order the company to reduce it. No hard numbers. No bright lines. OSFI decides.

A few things worth noting. OSFI needs prudential grounds to act — it's not unlimited authority. And certain assets are off the table for divestment orders: government-guaranteed obligations, bank deposits, widely distributed debt, and obligations of entities the company controls.

One more thing — Division 11 isn't in force yet. It comes into effect on a date set by Order in Council, and that date hasn't been set.

What this means practically: the hard limits that used to anchor investment policy are gone. Risk frameworks that relied on those statutory ceilings now need to be built around OSFI's supervisory expectations instead — which means paying closer attention to guidance, examinations, and how OSFI is actually thinking about these portfolios.

---

**Division 12 — Electronic Policyholder Communications**

Short one. Insurers can now send governance documents — meeting notices, proxy materials — to policyholders electronically without getting consent first. Previously you needed it. Now you just follow the notice of availability requirements, keep documents online for a year, and provide paper copies on request. Mostly an ops efficiency story.

---

**Division 13 — Widely Held Threshold**

The widely held rule says that once an insurer's equity hits a certain level, no single person or group can hold a controlling interest. The threshold just doubled — from $2 billion to $4 billion — for both insurers and holding companies.

So if you're sitting between $2B and $4B in equity, concentrated ownership is now on the table in a way it wasn't before. For anyone thinking about ownership structure or governance in that range, it's worth a look.

---

**Division 14 — OSFI Supervisory Powers**

Three things, all effective immediately on Royal Assent.

First, OSFI's annual examination now has to include an assessment of whether a company has adequate policies to protect against threats to its integrity and security — and whether it actually follows them. Cybersecurity is now a formal exam criterion, not just an afterthought.

Second, OSFI can now issue compliance directions for inadequate security policies alone — separate from any finding of unsafe or unsound practice. You can be financially sound and still get a direction if OSFI thinks your security framework isn't up to scratch.

Third, OSFI can now share supervisory information with any federal agency for purposes related to regulation, threats to financial institution integrity, or national security. That national security piece is new.

---

**Closing**

The thread running through all of this — especially Divisions 11 and 14 — is a move away from hard rules toward OSFI judgment. The 1991 Act was built on statutory certainty. C-15 is clearly moving in a different direction. More discretion for the regulator, more ambiguity for everyone else. For finance and risk teams, that's probably the most important thing to take away.

Happy to get into any of it.
