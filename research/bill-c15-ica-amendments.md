## Division 10 — Sunset Provision Extension

**What it does:** Extends the statutory deadline by which federally regulated insurers must be reauthorized by Parliament to continue operating. This is routine housekeeping — the ICA requires periodic renewal or insurers technically lose their licence.

| | Before | After |

| Insurance holding companies (s. 707) | Prior expiry date | **June 30, 2033** |

## Division 11 — Modernizing Prudential Limits on Borrowing, Loans and Investments

This is the most substantive division. It repeals an entire framework of hard statutory portfolio limits and replaces them with discretionary OSFI oversight.

### What Was Repealed and Why It Existed

---

#### Sections 473 (life companies) and 476 (P&C/marine companies) — Debt and Borrowing Limits

These sections prohibited insurers from entering into debt obligations or issuing non-common shares if the combined total of their debt obligations and stated capital would exceed a **prescribed percentage of total assets** (set by regulation).

In plain terms: a hard statutory ceiling on how leveraged an insurer could become through borrowing. Both life companies (s. 473) and P&C/marine companies (s. 476) had their own version.

**Repealed entirely.** No replacement. OSFI now relies on its capital adequacy frameworks (LICAT for life companies, MCT for P&C) rather than a statutory debt ceiling.

---

#### Definition of "Commercial Loan" in s. 490(1) — Anchor Definition

This defined what counted as a "commercial loan" for purposes of the lending limits. It **excluded**:
- Loans to natural persons of $250,000 or less
- Loans to federal, provincial, municipal, or foreign governments
- Government-guaranteed loans
- Mortgages on residential property (up to 80% LTV)
- Mortgages on commercial real property (with conditions)

In other words, a commercial loan was essentially: **any loan to a business that wasn't government-backed or secured by real property.** This definition triggered the limits in ss. 503–508. **Repealed** because the limits it served no longer exist.

---

#### Section 503 — Life Company Commercial Lending Limit (≤$25M regulatory capital)

> *"A life company that has twenty-five million dollars or less of regulatory capital shall not... make or acquire a commercial loan... if the aggregate value of all commercial loans held by the company and its prescribed subsidiaries exceeds... **5 per cent of the total assets of the company.**"*

Small life companies were subject to a hard 5% of total assets cap on commercial loans. **Repealed.**

---

#### Section 504 — Life Company Commercial Lending (>$25M regulatory capital)

Larger life companies (or those controlled by larger financial institutions) could exceed the 5% cap — but only with **prior written approval from the Superintendent**, who could impose terms and conditions. Not a hard cap, but OSFI-gated. **Repealed** — the entire approval mechanism is gone.

---

#### Section 505 — P&C and Marine Company Lending Limit

P&C and marine companies could not hold commercial loans **or loans to natural persons** exceeding a **prescribed percentage of total assets** (set by regulation, higher than the life company limit). Note the inclusion of natural person loans — P&C companies sometimes make personal loans in certain lines of business. **Repealed.**

---

#### Section 506 — Real Property Investment Limit

All companies were prohibited from acquiring interests in real property or making improvements if the **aggregate value of all real property interests** would exceed **an amount set by regulation**. This capped how much of its balance sheet an insurer could put into buildings, land, and improvements — illiquid assets prone to valuation uncertainty. **Repealed.**

---

#### Section 507 — Equity Investment Limit

All companies were prohibited from acquiring participating shares or ownership interests in non-subsidiary entities if the **aggregate value of all such equity holdings** would exceed **an amount set by regulation**. This limited portfolio equity exposure beyond the insurer's own subsidiaries and permitted investments. **Repealed.**

---

#### Section 508 — Aggregate Limit (the catch-all)

Even if a company was within its individual limits on real property (s. 506) and equity (s. 507) separately, it could not exceed the **combined aggregate** of real property plus equity beyond **an amount set by regulation**. This prevented regulatory arbitrage between asset categories. **Repealed entirely.**

---

#### Section 542.1 — Fraternal Benefit Society Borrowing Limit

The same structure as ss. 473/476, applied to fraternal benefit societies (e.g., Knights of Columbus, Foresters Financial): prohibited societies from entering debt obligations or issuing non-common shares if the combined total would exceed a **prescribed percentage of total assets**. **Repealed.**

---

#### Sections 562, 563, 565, 566 — Society Lending, Real Property, Equity, and Aggregate Limits

A complete parallel set of limits for fraternal benefit societies:

- **s. 562** — lending limit (commercial loans + natural person loans): **prescribed % of total assets**
- **s. 563** — real property limit: **amount set by regulation**
- **s. 565** — equity limit: **amount set by regulation**
- **s. 566** — aggregate catch-all (real property + equity combined): **amount set by regulation**

All **repealed.**

---

#### Sections 616–620 — Foreign Company (Branch) Limits

For foreign insurers operating Canadian branches, the same limits applied but were calculated against **Canadian assets** rather than global assets:

- **s. 616** — foreign life company commercial lending: **prescribed % of Canadian assets** (split by insurance class for composite companies)
- **s. 617** — foreign P&C/marine company commercial lending: **prescribed % of Canadian assets**
- **s. 618** — foreign company real property: **prescribed % of Canadian assets** by class
- **s. 619** — foreign company equity: **prescribed % of Canadian assets**
- **s. 620** — foreign company aggregate catch-all (real property + equity combined)

All **repealed.**

---

#### Sections 978–983 — Insurance Holding Company Limits

A direct mirror of the company-level provisions, applied at the holding company level:

- **s. 978** — IHC debt and borrowing limit: **prescribed % of total assets**
- **s. 979** — IHC commercial lending limit (≤$25M reg. cap.): **5% of total assets**
- **s. 980** — IHC commercial lending above 5% (>$25M): required **Superintendent approval**
- **s. 981** — IHC real property limit: **amount set by regulation**
- **s. 982** — IHC equity limit: **amount set by regulation**
- **s. 983** — IHC aggregate catch-all (real property + equity combined)

All **repealed.**

---

### What Replaces the Repealed Limits

OSFI now has discretionary **divestment order** powers. If the Superintendent determines a company's portfolio of commercial loans, real property, or equity investments poses a prudential risk, OSFI can order the company to reduce that exposure within a reasonable period. However:

- There is **no statutory ceiling** — OSFI exercises judgment case by case
- OSFI can only act on **prudential grounds** — it must justify the order
- The following asset categories are **exempt from divestment orders**:
  - Government-guaranteed debt obligations (federal, provincial, municipal, foreign sovereign)
  - Debt fully secured by deposits at any financial institution
  - Widely distributed debt obligations (as defined by regulation)
  - Debt obligations of entities controlled by the insurer itself

**Coming into force:** Division 11 does not come into force on royal assent — it comes into force on a **date to be fixed by order of the Governor in Council.**

## Division 12 — Electronic Document Delivery to Policyholders

**What changed:** Insurers may now send governance documents (meeting notices, proxy circulars, etc.) to policyholders **electronically without prior consent**, provided they:
- Send a notice of availability following securities rules (NI 51-102 / NI 54-101)
- Keep documents available online for at least one year in accessible, printable, searchable format
- Provide paper copies within 3 business days (before meeting) or 10 days (after meeting) upon request

| | Before | After |
|---|---|---|
| Default delivery method | Paper | Electronic |
| Consent required for electronic delivery | Yes | No |
| Right to receive paper copies | N/A | Yes, on request |

## Division 13 — Public Holding (Widely Held) Threshold

**What the public holding requirement is:** Once an insurer's equity exceeds the threshold, it must have broadly distributed public ownership — no single person or group can hold a controlling interest. This is the "widely held" rule designed to prevent concentration of ownership in large financial institutions.

**What changed:** Every reference to "two" (billion) in six ICA sections replaced by "four" (billion).

| | Before | After |
|---|---|---|
| Equity threshold (companies — ss. 411, 416) | **$2 billion** | **$4 billion** |
| Equity threshold (IHCs — ss. 938, 943) | **$2 billion** | **$4 billion** |
| Related formula thresholds (ss. 495, 971) | **$2 billion** | **$4 billion** |

**Practical effect:** Insurers with equity between $2B and $4B are no longer required to be widely held. Their owners have more flexibility to concentrate ownership without triggering the public holding requirement.

## Division 14 — OSFI Supervisory Powers

Three distinct expansions of OSFI's authority, all effective on royal assent.

---

### 1. Annual Examination Mandate Expanded (s. 674)

OSFI's annual examination of each company must now assess whether the company:

> *"has adequate policies and procedures to protect itself against threats to its integrity or security and adheres to those policies and procedures."*

Security posture — cybersecurity, operational integrity, anti-fraud infrastructure — is now a formal examination criterion alongside financial soundness.

---

### 2. Directions of Compliance Expanded (s. 676)

Previously, OSFI could issue compliance directions only if a company was committing an **unsafe or unsound practice**. Now OSFI has a separate, explicit power to issue directions when a company:

> *"does not have adequate policies and procedures to protect itself against threats to its integrity or security or does not adhere to those policies and procedures."*

This is a distinct trigger — a company can be financially sound and not engaged in any unsafe practice, yet still receive a compliance direction if OSFI considers its security framework inadequate.

---

### 3. Information Sharing with Federal Agencies (ss. 672, 998)

OSFI may now disclose supervisory information to **any federal government agency or body** for purposes related to:
- Regulation or supervision of financial institutions
- **Threats to the integrity or security of financial institutions**
- **Risks to national security**

Previously, information sharing was more restricted. The national security carve-out is new and notable — likely a response to concerns about foreign ownership of financial institutions, cyberattacks, and financial system security.

---

## Overall Assessment

The philosophical shift across Divisions 11 and 14 is significant and consistent: the 1991 ICA was built on **prescriptive rules** — hard numbers, statutory certainty, bright lines. The 2026 amendments move toward a **principles-based** supervisory model where OSFI exercises judgment rather than enforcing formulas. This aligns with the trajectory of modern financial regulation globally (Basel III, IFRS 17, Solvency II) and gives OSFI considerably more discretion — and responsibility — in managing the risk profile of the Canadian insurance sector.
