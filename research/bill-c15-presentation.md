# Bill C-15 — ICA Amendments
## 4-Minute Presentation | Insurance Finance & Risk Audience

---

**Opening**

Bill C-15, the Budget 2025 Implementation Act, received Royal Assent on March 26, 2026. Four divisions of the Act amend the Insurance Companies Act in ways that matter directly to how Canadian insurers are regulated, governed, and supervised. I'll walk through each.

---

**Division 10 — Sunset Extension**

The ICA requires periodic parliamentary reauthorization for federally regulated insurers to continue operating. Division 10 extends that deadline to June 30, 2033, for both insurance companies and insurance holding companies. This is routine housekeeping — no operational impact — but it removes a near-term legislative cliff that would otherwise have required attention.

---

**Division 11 — Investment and Lending Limits**

This is the most substantive change in the Act.

Prior to C-15, the ICA contained a framework of hard statutory limits on borrowing, commercial lending, real property investment, and equity investment. These applied to life companies, P&C and marine companies, fraternal benefit societies, foreign branch insurers, and insurance holding companies. Every category had its own prescribed ceilings — set either directly in the statute or by regulation.

All of those limits have been repealed.

What replaces them is a principles-based supervisory model. OSFI now has discretionary authority to issue divestment orders if it determines that a company's portfolio of commercial loans, real property, or equity investments poses a prudential risk. There are no statutory ceilings — OSFI exercises judgment on a case-by-case basis.

A few things to note. OSFI must have prudential grounds to act — it cannot issue orders arbitrarily. Certain asset classes are exempt from divestment orders entirely: government-guaranteed obligations, deposits at financial institutions, widely distributed debt, and obligations of controlled entities.

Also important: Division 11 does not come into force on Royal Assent. It takes effect on a date to be fixed by Order in Council. That date has not yet been set.

The practical implication for finance and risk professionals is a shift from rule-following to judgment. Investment mandates and risk frameworks that were previously governed by hard ICA limits will now need to be managed against OSFI's supervisory expectations — which will evolve through guidance, examination, and engagement rather than statutory text.

---

**Division 12 — Electronic Policyholder Communications**

A simpler change. Insurers can now send governance documents — meeting notices, proxy circulars, and similar materials — electronically to policyholders without requiring prior consent. The previous default was paper delivery.

The conditions: documents must follow securities-equivalent notice of availability requirements, remain accessible online for at least one year, and be provided in paper form within prescribed timeframes upon request. For most companies this is an operational efficiency gain, not a risk issue.

---

**Division 13 — Widely Held Threshold**

The widely held threshold — the equity level above which an insurer must maintain broadly distributed public ownership — has been doubled from two billion to four billion dollars. This applies to both direct insurers and insurance holding companies.

The effect is that insurers with equity between two and four billion dollars are no longer required to be widely held. Concentrated ownership structures that were previously prohibited in that range are now permissible. For governance and ownership risk analysis, this bracket warrants attention.

---

**Division 14 — OSFI Supervisory Powers**

Three expansions, all effective on Royal Assent.

First, OSFI's annual examination mandate now explicitly includes assessing whether a company has adequate policies and procedures to protect itself against threats to its integrity and security — and whether it adheres to them. Cybersecurity and operational integrity are now formal examination criteria alongside financial soundness.

Second, OSFI's power to issue directions of compliance has been expanded. Previously, a direction required a finding of unsafe or unsound practice. Now OSFI has a separate, explicit trigger: inadequate security policies or failure to adhere to them — independent of financial soundness.

Third, OSFI may now share supervisory information with any federal government agency for purposes related to regulation and supervision, threats to the integrity or security of financial institutions, or risks to national security. The national security carve-out is new.

---

**Closing**

Taken together, the C-15 amendments reflect a deliberate move away from the prescriptive, rules-based structure of the original 1991 Act toward a principles-based supervisory model — more OSFI discretion, fewer statutory bright lines. For finance and risk teams, that means closer engagement with OSFI's evolving expectations will matter more than it did before.
